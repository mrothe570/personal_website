FAQ

So what exactly is your design philosophy?

I believe in a supplementary, accessible web. A supplementary web experience, to me, does as much as it can to guide the user along their journey, whatever that may be, without worrying as much about engagement.

Individual autonomy is important. In the age of interstital pop-ups, endless notifications, dark patterns, and on and on, it's important for people to feel like the things they encounter while using the web are working with them.

Reflecting your personal philosophy in the work that you do can be tricky, but for me it boils down to a few things:

  • • designing apps and pages with contrast and readability as the main consideration
  • • prioritizing fast-loading, low-data-impact formatting
  • • applying consistent and thoughtful color psychology concepts
  • • keeping frictionless experiences frictionless, but adding friction where it's necessary

The first three points are somewhat universal for designers, but the last is something of my own rumination. But it goes back to the idea that user autonomy is key and should be one of the foundational considerations of any well-designed web experience.

A social media site like Twitter, for instance, features its feed. Which, as we all know, is a curated scroll consisting of everyone you follow and all of their engagements on Twitter. If just a little bit of friction were added to this experience, like requiring users to enter the Twitter handle of the account whose content they wish to view, users could spend a lot less time browsing Twitter and Facebook and a lot more of doing what they got onto their device for.

What is your background?

Before self-teaching with freeCodeCamp and Colt Steele's Web Developer Bootcamp 2021, I was a student at Missouri State University studying professional and technical writing. I did not finish my degree while at MSU, but have continued to focus on applying the concepts I learned for page design and technical writing to my work doing web design.

I've worked for different local businesses here in Springfield doing social media work in different capacities, and even ran a few campaigns for Missouri State University's Career Center. These mostly involved different Career Center events on campus, targeted at students within the different schools at MSU. I became more focused on web design after some work done with Lionbridge Technologies in the capacity of Rater, which led to some research into common SEO techniques and practices like page optimization and keyword search term ranking, which finally led into crafting modern websites and my own design phliosophy.

Finally, I've made a point to brush up on the more foundational aspects of design. Concepts like color theory, typography composition, the golden ratio, and grid theory are the most basic concepts when it comes to design. A strong foundational knowledge in these areas will serve anyone who wants to create great user experiences with informed design decisions.

Learning these concepts also taught me some of the more basic concepts from the most popular graphic design programs:
